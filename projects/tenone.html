Ten One Teahouse

Connecting with users by storyboarding their boba journey

Role

UX researcher

Timeline

March 2025

Skills

Persona Creation • Storyboarding • User Observation

Overview

For boba lovers like me, walking down Thayer Street every day is dangerous: in less than 3 blocks, I pass 4 entirely different boba shops. And after trying each one (as any boba lover would do), I noticed that while each had its distinct vibe and menu, they all shared the same self-checkout kiosk.

After this first revelation, I started to see the kiosk everywhere: poke shops, acai stores, pretty much anywhere I went for a quick snack. I wondered— what made these kiosks so ubiquitous? Why are they so appealing to users, and is there anything we can improve to make their experience more efficient and intuitive? In this case study, I try to answer those questions by creating personas and a storyboard for users at my favorite boba shop —Ten One Tea House!

Problem

How can we improve the self-checkout kiosk experience for Ten One Teahouse customers?

Illustrations of kiosk's interaction capabilities

User Research

To improve the user experience of the kiosk, I first had to understand the users themselves.

I conducted two forms of user research: naturalistic observation and user interviews, to understand:

  1. How users interacted with the kiosk
  2. What users liked and disliked about the kiosk
  3. Users' goals when using the kiosk

Observations

Unsurprisingly, given the primary demographic of college students, there didn't seem to be much confusion with the mechanics of the kiosk. There was however a good range of interaction frequencies and durations—some users would take their time clicking in and out of menu items, while others would find what they were looking for quickly. However, one surprising commonality was that almost all users would multitask while at the kiosk.

Interviews

After observing for a while, I chose three users to interview. I created the following survey to gather insights on users' experiences while using the kiosk:

Questions

  1. Why did you use the kiosk instead of ordering at the counter?
  2. How easy was it to find the features you were looking for on a scale of 1-5? Could you explain your rating?
  3. What were the most useful features?
  4. What were the least useful features?
  5. How clear were the kiosk’s instructions on a scale of 1-5? Could you explain your rating?
  6. Did you find any elements of the kiosk confusing or unnecessary? If so, can you describe them?
  7. Are there any changes that would make the kiosk easier to use?

Responses

Personas

After compiling my notes on users' kiosk interactions, I created two personas to capture the common behaviors, needs, frustrations, and goals I observed in order to help me empathize with users and base design decisions off real-world observations.

Storyboarding

Armed with my personas, I created a storyboard to visualize Amanda's potential user's journey through the kiosk, from when she steps up to the kiosk to when she receives her drink.

Though creating the personas were great for getting a general idea of Amanda as a user, drawing out this storyboard allowed me to concretely personify her actions and reactions to the interface —crucial steps for truly understanding a user's experience.

Conclusion

Findings

The kiosk I explored in this case study was already pretty optimized—surprisingly, more users had issues with the physical rather than digital aspects of the interface.

However, based on the data gathered from my user research process, I would like to explore some potential changes: allowing users to save favorite drinks and clearly indicating which drinks are customizable. I'd also like to try streamlining the workflow inefficiencies a couple users mentioned.

Next Steps

I'd like to continue this case study by implementing my proposed changes. I'd use the results from this research to create a new user flow, then conduct A/B testing to get feedback on the new design, repeating the user research process if necessary.
